# Assumptions Register

Every simplifying assumption in the simulation, in one place. Read this before
trusting any number the app produces. Each entry notes what to keep in mind
when making real decisions.

| # | Assumption | Keep in mind |
|---|------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Annual timestep.** All flows, taxes, and returns are yearly; flows occur at the start of the year, growth applies at the end. | Intra-year sequence effects and dollar-cost averaging are invisible. "Age 59½" is modeled as the year you turn 60; "age 65" (HSA) as the year you turn 65. |
| 2 | **Simulate nominal, display real.** All inputs are in today's (2026) dollars; the engine inflates them per path and deflates outputs for "real" views. | Real-dollar displays depend on each path's simulated inflation, not a fixed deflator. |
| 3 | **Bootstrap mode (default)** resamples joint annual rows (stock, bond, inflation) from Shiller 1871–2022 with ~5-year stationary blocks. | History may not contain the future. The dataset ends at 2022 (Shiller's bond series limit). Cash has no historical series: cash return = sampled inflation + cash real CAGR. |
| 4 | **Parametric mode** draws IID lognormal real returns per asset, independent across assets, with AR(1) inflation. | Misses stock–bond–inflation correlation regimes (e.g. 2022 when both fell). Use bootstrap when correlation matters. |
| 5 | **Return inputs are real CAGR** (geometric), not arithmetic means. | Entering an arithmetic mean (~1–1.5% higher for stocks) silently overstates outcomes. |
| 6 | **Inflation is AR(1)** (mean 2.5%, persistence 0.65, σ 1.2% by default). | Captures persistence (1970s-style runs) but not hyperinflation tails. |
| 7 | **Scheduled crashes only.** Crash events replace that year's sampled return; no random crash injection. | Random crashes on top of historically calibrated returns would double-count disasters. Use crash events as deterministic stress tests. |
| 8 | **Federal tax = ordinary brackets + standard deduction + LTCG stacking, single filer.** No AMT, NIIT, itemizing, or credits. State = flat rate on federal taxable income. FICA = 6.2% to the wage base + 1.45% uncapped. | High earners in high-tax states are understated. The flat state rate (default 5%) taxes capital gains the federal 0% bracket spares — that is realistic for most states, incl. Wisconsin (its 30% LTCG exclusion is ignored). |
| 9 | **Brackets, deductions, and contribution limits grow with each path's simulated inflation** (no $500-step rounding), frozen at 2026 law (post-OBBBA). | Refresh `engine/fire_engine/data/*.json` each November. Major tax reform invalidates projections. |
| 10 | **Account types merge into five pools** (taxable, traditional, Roth, HSA, cash) with a **single global asset allocation** across all invested pools; the cash pool earns the cash return. Annual rebalancing is frictionless and tax-free. | Per-account granularity below the pool level is not tax-relevant. Asset-location efficiency (bonds in tax-advantaged) is not modeled. Real-world taxable rebalancing can realize gains. |
| 11 | **Taxable account drag**: average cost basis, aggregate lot; a flat 2% qualified dividend yield taxed annually (reinvested into basis). Bond/cash interest inside the taxable pool is treated as price return (untaxed until sale); only the cash pool's interest is taxed as ordinary income. | No tax-loss harvesting or specific-lot upside. Dividend yield is configurable. |
| 12 | **Contributions require wages** (IRS compensation rule) and are capped by wages; Roth IRA income phase-outs are ignored (backdoor Roth assumed available); traditional contributions assumed fully deductible; employer match = match% × salary whenever you contribute anything to a 401k, not counted against the employee limit. | The 415(c) total-additions limit is not enforced. HSA contributions are also gated on wages (and assume HDHP coverage continues). |
| 13 | **Withdrawal rules**: penalty-free at 60 (for 59½); Roth ordering contributions → seasoned conversions → earnings; conversions season after 5 calendar years; optional last-resort early traditional withdrawals pay the 10% penalty. Rule of 55 and 72(t)/SEPP are not modeled. | The ladder + taxable bridge is the supported early-access strategy. A path "fails" the first year spending cannot be met from accessible sources. |
| 14 | **RMDs start at 75** (SECURE 2.0, born 1960+) using the Uniform Lifetime Table, regardless of birth year entered. | Slightly wrong for anyone born before 1960 (their RMD age is 73). |
| 15 | **Social Security** = your ssa.gov FRA estimate × claiming-age factor (62–70, FRA 67) × haircut, inflation-indexed. Benefits are taxed via the IRS provisional-income worksheet (single filer): the taxable share steps 0% → 50% → 85% across the $25,000 / $34,000 thresholds, where provisional income = all non-SS income (ordinary + capital gains) + ½ of benefits. These two thresholds are **not** inflation-indexed in statute, so the engine compares nominal income against them unscaled — modeling the "tax torpedo" worsening over a long retirement. | The ssa.gov statement assumes you keep earning until claiming — retiring early means the true benefit is lower than the statement; the haircut knob must absorb that too. Because the thresholds never inflate, late-retirement years drift toward the 85% cap regardless of strategy. No tax-exempt (muni) interest in the provisional base; MFJ thresholds not modeled (single filer only). |
| 16 | **HSA**: utilization × HSA-eligible expenses paid tax-free from the HSA each year; an optional cash buffer (today's $) stays uninvested at the cash return while the rest earns the blended allocation return; at 65+, non-medical withdrawals are penalty-free ordinary income. No receipt-shoeboxing (deferred reimbursement). | Shoebox strategies would make the HSA strictly better than modeled. |
| 17 | **Out-of-pocket healthcare** (deductibles, copays, dental) is an expense stream with its own extra inflation rate. Pre-65 ACA premium subsidies and 65+ IRMAA surcharges are now modeled separately — see #26 and #27. | Keep marketplace premiums in the ACA inputs (#26), not in an expense stream, or you'll double-count. |
| 18 | **Spending**: constant real per stream by default; optional **Guyton-Klinger-style guardrails** flex discretionary (non-essential) streams when the withdrawal rate drifts ±band around its value at retirement (cut/restore steps, floor/cap bounds, evaluated annually against start-of-year portfolio). One-time and windfall event amounts are in today's dollars; windfalls arrive after-tax. Non-inflating streams (e.g. a mortgage) stay fixed in nominal dollars. | With guardrails off, failure rates are pessimistic (real people cut spending in bad markets). With them on, success assumes you actually execute the cuts — typically +5–15pp at marginal withdrawal rates. |
| 19 | **Single filer, fixed horizon** (default age 90), no mortality table, no spouse. | Marriage is a future schema upgrade; longevity risk is handled by choosing a conservative horizon. |
| 20 | **Coast FIRE math is deterministic**: 25× expenses at the blended real CAGR. The MC-derived FIRE number is the bisected portfolio scale where retiring *today* meets the success threshold. | The 25× number is intuition; trust the MC number for decisions — at long horizons the safe multiple is usually higher than 25×. |
| 21 | **Nominal raises convert at expected inflation**: a nominal salary growth input g becomes real growth (1+g)/(1+mean inflation)−1, and the salary then tracks each path's *realized* inflation around that real trend. | In a path with surprise inflation, the model assumes your employer keeps up (plus/minus your real trend) — real-world salaries often lag inflation spikes. |
| 22 | **Liabilities are deterministic nominal amortizations**: the fixed annual payment is an essential, non-inflating expense until balance × (1 + rate) − payment reaches zero; the outstanding balance is subtracted from reported net worth but never triggers failure by itself (only the payment can). No refinancing, no early payoff, no variable rates. | Loan payments are nominal contracts, so inflation erodes them — that is modeled. Selling the financed asset (house equity) is not: enter a windfall event for that. |
| 23 | **Everything unspent is saved**: each year's surplus after taxes, expenses, and loan payments flows down the contribution waterfall; whatever the waterfall does not capture pools in the cash account at the cash return. There is no "lifestyle absorbs the rest" leak. | The Annual Investing chart shows where the plan thinks every surplus dollar goes — if its Cash band is large, the waterfall is not capturing your real savings behavior. |
| 24 | **The retirement-age sweep holds the event timeline fixed**: events stay pinned to their calendar age while the retirement age varies. A New Salary event dated after a candidate retirement age therefore means "return to work at that age" (barista FIRE), which can make the success curve non-monotonic. Years-to-FI uses the earliest age whose success stays at/above the threshold for all later ages. | One-time expenses are always accounted at every candidate retirement age; only income-restoring events create peaks. |
| 25 | **Spending snapshots use a fixed, add-only category taxonomy** (permanent slugs; display names and order freely editable; never rename/merge/split a slug). Amounts are nominal $/yr at the snapshot date; charts convert to today's dollars using the assumed mean inflation, not realized CPI. Sinking-fund contributions count as spending in their category at contribution time (never again when spent). Insurance = premiums actually paid out of pocket, ignoring tax character and employer subsidy. Income/payroll taxes and loan payments are excluded (tracked by the engine and the Liabilities section); property tax belongs in Home. | The real-dollar view drifts from truth if actual CPI diverges from the assumed mean — bundle realized CPI later if precision matters. New categories may be added any time; their series simply start late. |
| 26 | **ACA premium subsidy (pre-65, optional).** When enabled, marketplace premiums use the post-2021 (IRA-extended) subsidy: the expected contribution is a piecewise-linear share of MAGI (0% below 150% FPL, rising to a flat 8.5% cap above 400% FPL — **no cliff**), and the subsidy is `benchmark − expected_contribution`, capped at your actual premium. Keyed to one user-supplied benchmark (SLCSP) and one actual premium; FPL is the single-filer base, inflated per path. MAGI ≈ ordinary income + capital gains + full Social Security. Applies only once retired and before `coverage_end_age` (65); co-converges with taxes/withdrawals in the annual fixed point. | Single-filer only; one flat benchmark instead of a rating-area × age table, so it captures the *mechanism* (and its coupling to Roth conversions, which raise MAGI and shrink the subsidy) more than the exact dollar. Don't also keep a manual pre-65 premium in your expense streams, or you'll double-count. Out-of-pocket medical (deductibles, copays) still belongs in expense streams. |
| 27 | **IRMAA Medicare surcharge (65+, optional).** When enabled, a step-function Part B + D surcharge is added as an essential cost above each MAGI bracket (single-filer 2025 tiers by default, editable, inflated per path). MAGI ≈ AGI (ordinary income + capital gains + taxable SS). | Real IRMAA lags **two years** (your premium keys off MAGI from two years prior); the engine uses current-year MAGI as a simplification, so a one-off conversion spike shows its surcharge the same year rather than two years later. The conversion/RMD marginal-rate readouts deliberately do **not** fold in IRMAA, to avoid double-penalizing a ladder for a lagged cost. |
| 28 | **Tax-regime / TCJA-sunset stress (optional, on-demand).** Re-runs the plan as if today's law reverts at a chosen age: ordinary bracket *rates* scaled (default ×1.15) and the standard deduction scaled (default ×0.5) from that age forward, on the same market paths. The conversion ladder keeps filling to the same *taxable-income* ceiling; LTCG brackets, FICA, and the SS provisional thresholds are left unchanged. | A flat rate-multiplier is an approximation of an actual pre-2018 bracket table — read the direction and rough magnitude, not the exact dollar. It surfaces the largest decades-scale policy risk to the low-bracket ladder thesis, which is otherwise invisible because every other view assumes 2026 law holds for the whole horizon. |
| 29 | **Spending strategy (decumulation rule).** Default `constant_dollar` funds the plan's expense streams, optionally flexed by the guardrails (#18). Optional portfolio-percentage rules set *discretionary* spending from the current balance instead: `constant_pct` (fixed % of balance), `vpw` (% rises with age via an annuity payout factor using an assumed real return, drawing the balance down by the horizon), and `floor_ceiling` (constant_pct clamped to a floor/ceiling × planned discretionary). In every percentage mode, essentials (medical + loan payments) are funded first and a path still fails if the portfolio can't cover them; the percentage applies only to the discretionary remainder. Distinct from the Withdrawal Policy, which only orders *which account* is tapped. | The percentage rules can spend well above plan in strong markets (that's the point of VPW); they never deplete to zero on discretionary, but they do not protect essentials. The guardrail cap can now exceed 100% so good markets can fund extra lifestyle — sensible given no bequest motive (#19). |

## Categorized Summaries

### Simulation Mechanics and Economic Models
The simulation operates on an annual timestep where all flows, taxes, and returns are calculated yearly. Flows occur at the start of the year, while growth applies at the end, meaning intra-year sequence effects and dollar-cost averaging are not modeled. All inputs are provided in today's dollars, but the engine inflates them per path and deflates outputs to present "real" views, which depend on each path's simulated inflation. The default bootstrap mode resamples joint annual rows of stock, bond, and inflation data from Shiller's 1871–2022 dataset using roughly 5-year stationary blocks. Alternatively, a parametric mode draws independent lognormal real returns per asset with AR(1) inflation, though this misses correlation regimes. Return inputs must be geometric real CAGRs rather than arithmetic means to avoid overstating outcomes. Inflation follows an AR(1) process with persistence, and market crashes are only modeled deterministically as scheduled events rather than randomly injected. Projections use a fixed horizon (default age 90) and assume a single filer. Coast FIRE calculations deterministically target a 25× multiple at the blended real CAGR, while Monte Carlo success rates provide the actual FI number. Lastly, the retirement-age sweep holds event timelines fixed to their calendar age, which can create non-monotonic success curves if events imply returning to work.

### Taxation and Policy Rules
The model applies federal tax based on ordinary brackets, the standard deduction, and long-term capital gains stacking for a single filer, excluding AMT, NIIT, itemized deductions, or credits. State tax is treated as a flat rate on federal taxable income, while FICA applies at 6.2% to the wage base plus an uncapped 1.45%. Tax brackets, deductions, and contribution limits inflate dynamically per path, frozen at 2026 tax law. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) are modeled to start at age 75 via the Uniform Lifetime Table. For healthcare, the model supports an optional ACA premium subsidy before age 65 (capturing the mechanism of the IRA-extended subsidy based on MAGI, but lacking a rating-area table) and an optional step-function IRMAA Medicare surcharge at 65+, which keys off current-year MAGI rather than the real-world two-year lag to avoid double-penalizing Roth conversion ladders.

### Accounts and Portfolio Management
Account types are merged into five distinct pools: taxable, traditional, Roth, HSA, and cash. A single global asset allocation applies across all invested pools, with annual frictionless and tax-free rebalancing, while the cash pool earns the cash return. The taxable account models tax drag using average cost basis, an aggregate lot, and a configurable flat qualified dividend yield taxed annually. Contributions are capped by and require wages, assuming full deductibility for traditional contributions and backdoor availability for Roths, with employer matches triggering upon any 401k contribution. Withdrawals follow standard penalty-free rules at age 60, ordering Roth withdrawals from contributions to seasoned conversions to earnings. Early access is supported via a conversion ladder and taxable bridge; Rule of 55 and 72(t) are not modeled.

### Income, Spending, and Liabilities
Spending is modeled as constant real amounts per stream by default, with optional Guyton-Klinger-style guardrails that flex discretionary spending based on withdrawal rates. Sinking-fund contributions count as spending when contributed, and snapshots use a fixed, add-only category taxonomy for tracking nominal expenses over time. Nominal salary raises convert to an expected real growth trend, but track realized inflation per path. Liabilities are handled as deterministic nominal amortizations where inflation erodes the real burden, but refinancing or variable rates are unsupported. Social Security benefits are based on your ssa.gov FRA estimate, inflation-indexed, and taxed via the single-filer provisional-income worksheet whose thresholds are not inflation-indexed. Healthcare is treated as an expense stream with its own inflation rate, while HSA utilization assumes eligible expenses are paid tax-free annually without receipt-shoeboxing. Finally, any unspent surplus flows down a contribution waterfall, with leftovers pooling in the cash account, meaning the simulation does not model lifestyle inflation absorbing extra cash.
